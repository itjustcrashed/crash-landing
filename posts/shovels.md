Shovels are a great tool. They've helped human society for millennia, and I'd never doubt their value to our species.

Shovels help children learn to use tools. Shovels are used to dig up literal treasure. Shovels can help construct a building. They can sometimes even just change the vibe of a room.

One of many things shovels cannot do, however, is neurosurgery.

Shovel companies could try to sell the idea of it to you, and a lot of the time, it may seem plausible. But you've gotta take a step back and take a look at reality. They're just far too large and dirty to _properly_ preform that medical operation. You can try all you want, but even if you somehow "succeed," it'll severely lower the quality of life for whoever had that neurosurgery.

A shovel wasn't designed for neurosurgery, and it shouldn't be used for that. They do so many great things, and have truly benefitted society for the good. We wouldn't have the world we have today without shovels; however, we can't bet the whole economy on them, and we can't replace everyone with a shovel. The whole world's supply of metal shouldn't go to shovel production to make shovels cheaper and more appealing, either.

Every object and person has its own unique purpose, and they're (usually) great at it. They can be adapted for other uses, but never blindly. So be careful what problems you try to solve with shovels.
